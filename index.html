Ancient Artifact Gripper

One of our projects for our Mechanical Engineering Design class. We were tasked with recovering an "ancient human artifact", an object that our professor picks every year. This year our artifact wasnthe worlds first fidget spinner. To be exact, it was actually a large aluminum fidget spinner dummy. Our mission was to build a gripper that would be strong enough to hold this artifact. Not only did it have to grip the object and lift it but, it also had to grip the object as it swung as a pendulum 3 times.




Our team's gripper had to fit under a motor. With certain geometric constraints vertically and horizontally. Additionally, we couldn't grip the object from the bottom, since it was considered a "restricted" zone. Additionally, the gripper would have to be able to swing down from the side for initial placement.



My group and I knew that our biggest challenge was going to translate the motor's torque to translational force and press against the sides of the fidget spinner and hold it while it swings. Thus we came up with a design that optimizes this conversion, as well as uses large bevel gears in order to be able to multiply our torque as well as translate our our motion to grip the fidget spinner.


Acrylic Mechanical Robot: G.O.A.T

Another project for our Design I project. This time we were tasked with creating a mechanical robot with a "starter kit" created by the course teaching staff. We are also provided with 3 different test stages. Rocks, stairs, and hurdles. We used all that data to design our robot to clear all the stages together.

Our procedure includes analyzing an animal in nature and then, modeling our robot after it. Our team chose the goat, due to its ability to climb rough terrain and stablity. We found a goat video online, and traced coupler curves of its movement. We then used MotionGen to model those coupler curves and ended up with a Roshaf-Crank Rocker, that simulated a goat walking.
